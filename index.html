Introduction¶

As the COVID-19 pandemic winds down and more people become fully-vaccinated, we reflect on the loss of countless human lives, as well as the collective grief of a nation mourning their loved ones. At the same time, our lives are slowly returning to the way they were before, as businesses, schools, and public gathering places re-open and remove the pandemic precautions we have become so used to following.

It is no secret that the United States was one of countries with the highest number of COVID infections and deaths, and as we move into the future, health officials are asking themselves one glaring question…

“What went wrong, and how can we better prepare for pandemics like this in the future?”

It is not an easy question to answer, and it is one that will be discussed, debated, and researched for years to come. Today, I want to lend my voice to that cause, and I will do so by shifting my perspective to one area. Politics.

The way the Trump administration handled the pandemic is a topic of much scrutiny, and rightly so. In the wake of a deadly virus outbreak, the people in charge should be held accountable for action (and inaction) taken on the government level to prevent the spread of the virus and save lives. However, this administrations response is notable in the way that it politicized an issue that for the greater good of the public probably should not have been politicized. From doubting the virus’s existence in the early days of the outbreak, to consistently undermining the effectiveness of the precautions in place, an argument could be made that the actions of the Trump administration actively encouraged followers to reject the guidance of health officials and carry on as if nothing were happening.

The question I want to ask is this. Is there is a connection between political affiliation and COVID outbreaks in the US? For example, were there more COVID cases in states that leaned Republican compared to states that leaned Democratic? It is a worthwhile topic to explore, as uncovering political roots in the spread of virus cases could be beneficial in determining where to prioritize prevention efforts when another pandemic arises. By doing this, I can also shed light on the data science pipeline by walking you through a real-world example of data science at work.

(Naturally, the politicization of any issue in government can be a hot topic, and the pandemic is no exception. I am choosing to investigate this interaction out of personal curiosity and to determine whether a connection exists, not to point a finger at one group or another.)

So, without further ado, let us begin with the first step in the data science pipeline: data collection.

Data collection/curation¶

Any good data scientist begins his work by collecting the data he or she wants to work with. This can take a variety of forms, from collecting the data personally through surveys, to downloading data that has been previously collected from another source. For this research, we will be doing the latter. To explore the relationship between COVID cases and political affiliation, we will need to combine data from a variety of sources. Specifically, we will be accessing the data from four separate sources and combining it into a data set we can work with. Those sources are...

  • United States COVID-19 Cases and Deaths by State over Time

    https://data.cdc.gov/Case-Surveillance/United-States-COVID-19-Cases-and-Deaths-by-State-o/9mfq-cb36/data
    This data table from the CDC compiles up to the date COVID-19 reports from states and federal jurisdictions, including total cases, total deaths, and more starting on January 20th, 2020. With over 32,000 entries, this data set will be our primary source of COVID-19 data relating to total cases and deaths. Since the entries in our table are sorted by state, we should be able to easily match entries in this table with political data. Speaking of which...

  • 2020 National Popular Vote Tracker

    https://cookpolitical.com/2020-national-popular-vote-tracker
    From the Cook Political Report, this data set compiles data related to the 2020 presidential election, specifically the number of votes cast for Trump and Biden, as well as what political party each state was called for. With some tidying, we should be able to easily match this data with our COVID-19 data from the CDC.

  • Annual Estimates of the Resident Population for the Nation and States

    https://www.census.gov/programs-surveys/popest/technical-documentation/research/evaluation-estimates/2020-evaluation-estimates/2010s-state-total.html
    When it comes to discussing COVID cases in individual states, it would probably be helpful to know the total population of each state. To do this, we download state population totals from the US Census Bureau. While these totals are estimates, they are as close as we will get to knowing how many people are in each state.

  • COVID-19 Vaccinations in the United States

    https://covid.cdc.gov/covid-data-tracker/#vaccinations
    Now that COVID vaccinations are being distributed to the public, it could be helpful to know how many people are getting the vaccine, as well as the percent of the population of each state that is fully vaccinated. Again, we can easily get up to the date data on this subject from the CDC.

It is worth noting that when I started on this project, I was only using data from the first two data sets. After performing some analysis, I realized it would be helpful to have access to the population and vaccination data located in the other two tables. This is a common practice in data science. Before analyzing our data, we might not know what kind of connections and trends we want to explore, and later we might have to return to our data collection phase to explore some aspect of our data further. While the data science pipeline might seem straightforward, backtracking to previous steps is sometimes required.

Before we start to write any code, we must first import the Python libraries we want to use. If you are interested in learning more or are confused by any of the functions I use, I have provided links to documentation in the Sources section at the end of the notebook. After importing the data we want to use, we move on to the next step in the data science pipeline, data management.

Data management/representation¶

Above I have printed the first few rows of the data sets mentioned before performing any sort of manipulation or tidying (hence the suffix "raw" on each set). If we were to use the data as it was now, it would not only be confusing to try and interpret, but it would also be difficult to perform analysis on. Tidying data can take many forms, including deleting rows and columns, reformatting entries, handling missing data, and much more. In this step, we will go from four messy data tables to two neat data sets that we can analyze using some of these techniques.

For our CDC COVID-19 data, we want to limit our entries to a single day so we can look at just the total cases and deaths from that day for each state, resulting in 50 entries in the table (later we will use more of the data from this table in a different set). We also want to remove some of the columns that we do not need, as well as remove entries that do not correspond to one of the 50 states. To make the table more readable, we will order the state abbreviations alphabetically and rename the columns.

Now that we have our 4 tidy data sets, we want to combine them into one single table we can work with. If you look at our four sets, you will notice that each of them have something in common, which is that each has a column corresponding to US states, whether it is the full state names or their abbreviations. This is important, as these columns give us a way to seamlessly combine our tables via similar data.

We combine our election data and CDC data by merging the two data sets together using our stateid column. After re-sorting the resulting table by full state name instead of abbreviations (and resetting the index), we can then use concat to "stick" our population and vaccination tables to the end of our main table. We do not need to use merge here since all tables have the same number of entries and are sorted the same way, so we can use concat without fear of having mismatched data.

Finally, we want to create a new column containing the total number of cases for each state over the total estimated population. This will give us a rough estimate of the percentage of each state's population that has been infected and will be beneficial when we perform our analysis later.

Now we have a neat, tidy data set that we can work with. However, this data only reflects a specific date in time. What if we want to look at the trend of COVID-19 cases throughout the year? In this case, we want to create a separate data table that reflects this trend. In a very broad sense, we are going to extract entries from our raw CDC data corresponding to the first of each month from March 2020 to March 2021 and display our total cases as the sum of all the Democratic states and Republican states, respectively. By doing this, we should be able to get a broad idea of the trend of our data as it relates to political affiliations.

# We create an empty data table with columns for date, total cases in Democratic states, and total cases in Republican states.
time_data = pd.DataFrame(columns = ['Date','Dem_Cases','Rep_Cases'])

# For each element in our date list, we perform the following...
for date in date_list:
    
    # Extract the corresponding entries for the current date.
    curr_month = cdc_data_raw.loc[cdc_data_raw['submission_date'] == date][['state','tot_cases']]

    # Since this data is being taken from the raw table, it needs to be sanitized similar to the way we sanitized our original table.
    for s in state_drop_list_cdc:
        curr_month = curr_month[curr_month.state != s]

    curr_month = curr_month.sort_values(['state'], ascending = True)
    curr_month = curr_month.rename(columns = {'state': 'stateid'})
    
    # We need to merge our election data so we know which entries correspond to Democratic and Republican states.
    curr_month = pd.merge(election_data, curr_month, on = 'stateid')
    curr_month = curr_month[['state','stateid', 'called','tot_cases']]
    
    # Calculate the sum of the total cases in Democratic and Republican states
    total_dem_cases = curr_month.loc[curr_month['called'] == 'D']['tot_cases'].sum()
    total_rep_cases = curr_month.loc[curr_month['called'] == 'R']['tot_cases'].sum()

    # Create an entry in our time_data table containing the date and total cases that we calculated above.
    time_data = time_data.append({'Date': date, 'Dem_Cases': total_dem_cases, 'Rep_Cases': total_rep_cases}, ignore_index = True)

When we just look at total number of cases, we can see that Democratic states have more cases than Republican states for most time periods. But we thought we would see the opposite, right?

At this point, it is worth pointing out some of the potential drawbacks to the method we are choosing. In this plot, we are ignoring a substantial factor: population. By just plotting total cases, we are ignoring the differences in population that states have. For example, a state that has a high population would most likely have more COVID cases than one that has a relatively low population. This model also ignores population density, as groups of people in closer proximity to each other in areas like cities would spread a virus faster than groups in more spread out, rural areas. Remember how many cases New York had in the first few months of the pandemic compared to other states?

So instead of looking at total cases on their own, we can express this data as a percentage of total population to compensate. This way, we can compare states with different populations because we will be evaluating the percentage of total population that has been infected with COVID, not the total number of cases themselves. To do this, we add two more columns to our time data corresponding to these percentages.

We are making a few assumptions here that are worth pointing out. One is that we assume that the population for each state has not changed from month to month over the course of 2020. When it comes to our total number of COVID cases, we also assume that one case corresponds to one member of that state's population (i.e., no one got COVID twice, and each COVID case belongs to a resident of the state it was reported in).

Arguments can also be made that using 2020's election results to label states Republican or Democrat is not the best approach. For example, some states were highly contested up until the very end of the election, and simply labeling them D or R might not fully reflect the attitude of the state's population at large. Also, using 2020 election results means our political data is specific to that election's candidates and the public's attitudes to those candidates. Not to mention, the results of the 2020 election were most likely swayed by the Trump administration's handling of the pandemic itself.

A better approach might be to look at the number of voters in each state that are registered as Democrat or Republican and label each state as whichever of those groups is larger. This was the approach I initially wanted to take, but after realizing that some states do not require voters to align with a party when registering to vote, along with the fact that data of this kind was difficult to find, I went with the election approach knowing the potential sacrifices in accuracy and reliability I was making.

Making tradeoffs and recognizing the potential shortcomings of the data and analysis you are doing is a large part of data science. There are always assumptions to be made, and without all the data in the world our models can never be perfect. What matters is whether we acknowledge those assumptions and make them clear when communicating our results.

Machine Learning¶

Now we are going to shift our perspective a bit and focus on machine learning. Machine learning is a very complex topic, and we will not go into too much detail in this section. Basically, we want to know if COVID cases and vaccination rates are good predictors for whether a state is blue or red, and machine learning can help us answer this question.

On a very high level, machine learning is the process of using algorithms to "train" a computer to detect trends in data and to make predictions based on that training. We will be using supervised machine learning, where we know the expected outcomes and compare them with the predicted outcomes given to us by the computer. The more predictions the computer gets right, the higher the accuracy of our model is.

If you want to learn more about machine learning, I have provided some resources below.

In [17]:
# We extract the columns we want to use in our machine learning algorithm.
ml_data = main_data[['Population_Vaccinated','Population_Infected','Election_Result']]

# Our predictors (Population_Vaccinated and Population_Infected).
X = ml_data.drop('Election_Result', axis = 1)

# Our expected output.
y = ml_data['Election_Result']

For this exercise we will be looking at two kinds of machine learning algorithms, k-NN or K Nearest Neighbors, and Decision Trees.

The k-NN algorithm assumes that similar data points are located "near" each other. The goal is to classify the points by calculating the distance from one point to a specified number of other points (our k).

Decision Trees more closely represent the decision making process that we make as humans. The computer makes choices of which branch to take, eventually resulting in an outcome.

These are very broad descriptions of these algorithms, and I have provided links below if you want to learn more about them.

Both algorithms, while different in their execution, are coded in roughly the same way. We create a Classifier object specific to the algorithm we want to use (we can also pass in arguments called hyperparameters to these classifiers to further optimize our algorithms, but for now we will use the default options). Then we will use a 10-fold cross validation to produce the accuracy of our models. The way this works is our data set is split into 10 different groups. One of these groups is assigned the role of the test group, while the rest are used as training groups. The training groups are used to train the algorithm, and the test group tests it to see how well it performs (hence the names test and train). This occurs 10 times, and after it is done, we can find the mean of the resulting scores to get a good estimate of the accuracy of our algorithms.

Is this the most practical use of machine learning techniques? Not really. Our data set is not very big (only 50 entries for 50 states), and COVID data probably is not the most reliable predictor for future election results. Regardless, machine learning is utilized extensively in today's data-heavy industries, and it is worth discussing when delving into data science.
